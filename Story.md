# Lets Start by Identifying the Real Problem

In 2013, containers began to gain momentum.
Software shifted from monolithic to distributed systems, but the infrastructure needed
to support that shift did not.

## Why Is This a Problem? An Analogy

### The Monolith

Imagine a small company with five employees.
Each person provides a service that contributes to the companys overall offering.

They sit in the same room.
They know what everyone else does.
They understand each others boundaries.
They communicate freely.

Security, access control, coordination, and trust are informal, but sufficient.
At this scale, the system works naturally.

### The Distributed

Now the company grows to 1000 employees.

People are spread across rooms, buildings, and countries.
Multiple teams perform the same functions for redundancy and scale.
Communication paths multiply.
Security, access control, and coordination become hard problems.

Yet the company no longer improvises.

There is standard infrastructure: organizational structures, HR systems, access policies,
communication tools, and operating models designed to scale to N employees.

Managing a 1000 person company is fundamentally different from managing five, but we know
how to do it.

### Back to Software

When software moved from monoliths to distributed systems, there was no equivalent
infrastructure.

Each project, and often each engineer, was forced to invent a proprietary solution for:
communication, security, discovery, scaling, and coordination.

Armed with decades of monolithic experience and little distributed experience, the
industry made mistakes, then repeated them.

Those mistakes are still being paid for today.
In trillions.

# The Radical Simplicity Hypothesis

A software architect asks, "What should be built?"
A software engineer asks, "How do we build it?"

In theory, these are different hats.
In practice, career paths eventually force them onto the same head.

Distinguishing between the two sounds simple, but it is one of the hardest skills in
software engineering.

An architect must think in abstractions, not technologies.
In interfaces and contracts, not implementations.
In decoupling and coherence, not frameworks and tools.

That requires something deeply counterintuitive:
when designing, you must temporarily forget your past technical experience.

When we fail to do this, the consequences are severe.
Organizations pay 100 times more in time and cost.
Engineers burn out under unnecessary complexity.
Work life balance erodes while teams fight problems that should not exist.

This is a classic lose lose.

## Radical Simplicity as a Counterforce

I came to believe that radical simplicity is the only way to flip the equation to win win.
